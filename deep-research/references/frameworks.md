# Research Frameworks Reference

This file contains analytical frameworks for structured research. Each framework includes: what it is, when to use it, how to apply it properly, and common mistakes to avoid.

Select 1-3 frameworks per research task using the selection logic in the main SKILL.md. Applying a framework poorly is worse than not using one at all — read the full instructions before applying.

## PESTEL Analysis {#pestel}

### What it is
Scans the macro-environment across 6 dimensions: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal. Reveals external forces that shape an industry or market.

### When to use
- Entering a new market or geography
- Evaluating regulatory risk
- Understanding why a market is growing or shrinking
- Any research where external forces matter more than internal capabilities

### How to apply it properly

For each dimension, answer TWO questions:
1. **Current state:** What forces exist right now that affect this space?
2. **Direction of travel:** Is this force strengthening, weakening, or stable? What's the trajectory?

**Political:**
- Government policies affecting the industry (federal, state, local)
- Political stability and upcoming elections that could shift policy
- Trade policies, tariffs, subsidies
- Lobbying activity and political sentiment toward the industry
- *Direction:* Is regulation increasing, decreasing, or shifting focus?

**Economic:**
- Market size and growth rate (with methodology)
- Consumer spending patterns in this category
- Insurance and reimbursement dynamics (for healthcare)
- Pricing pressure and margin trends
- Funding and investment activity (VC, PE, public markets)
- *Direction:* Is money flowing in or out? Is willingness to pay increasing?

**Social:**
- Consumer attitudes and awareness
- Demographic trends affecting demand
- Stigma or social acceptance factors
- Media coverage and public narrative
- Trust dynamics (who do consumers trust in this space?)
- *Direction:* Is social acceptance growing? Is awareness increasing?

**Technological:**
- Current technology stack and alternatives
- Telehealth/digital delivery capabilities
- AI and automation opportunities
- Platform and infrastructure dependencies
- *Direction:* Is technology making this easier/cheaper? What's the adoption curve?

**Environmental:**
- Sustainability pressures on the industry
- Physical environment factors (geography, climate affecting delivery)
- Remote/virtual vs. in-person dynamics
- *Direction:* Is the shift to virtual permanent or reverting?

**Legal:**
- Specific regulations (federal and state-level)
- Licensing requirements
- Compliance obligations (HIPAA, state telehealth laws, etc.)
- Pending legislation or regulatory proposals
- Enforcement patterns (what's enforced aggressively vs. loosely)
- *Direction:* Is the legal environment tightening, loosening, or getting more complex?

### Common mistakes
- Listing factors without explaining WHY they matter for this specific research question
- Treating all 6 dimensions equally (usually 2-3 dominate — weight accordingly)
- Ignoring direction of travel (a static snapshot is half as useful)
- Staying too generic ("the economy affects consumer spending" — specify HOW and HOW MUCH)

### Output format
Present as a narrative organized by the 2-3 most important dimensions first, not as a 6-section checklist. End with a "macro forces summary" — the 3-5 external forces that will most shape outcomes in this space.

## Porter's Five Forces {#porters-five-forces}

### What it is
Analyzes competitive dynamics through 5 structural forces that determine industry profitability and attractiveness.

### When to use
- Evaluating whether to enter a market
- Understanding why margins are high or low in a space
- Identifying where competitive pressure is coming from
- Assessing long-term defensibility of a position

### How to apply it properly

**1. Threat of New Entrants** (How easy is it to enter?)
- Capital requirements to enter
- Regulatory barriers (licenses, certifications, compliance costs)
- Technology barriers (proprietary tech, platform dependencies)
- Brand and trust barriers (how much does reputation matter?)
- Distribution/channel barriers (can you reach customers?)
- Rate each sub-factor: HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW barrier
- *The key question:* If this is profitable, what stops 10 competitors from appearing next year?

**2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers** (Who do you depend on?)
- In healthcare/telehealth: physicians, pharmacies, labs, technology vendors
- How concentrated are suppliers? (Few large players vs. many small ones)
- Switching costs to change suppliers
- Are suppliers also potential competitors?
- *The key question:* Could your suppliers squeeze you, and what leverage do you have?

**3. Bargaining Power of Buyers** (How much power do customers have?)
- Price sensitivity of customers
- Switching costs for customers
- How informed are buyers? (Transparent pricing vs. opaque)
- Do buyers have alternatives?
- Volume dynamics (many small customers vs. few large ones)
- *The key question:* Can customers force prices down or demands up?

**4. Threat of Substitutes** (What else solves the same problem?)
- Direct substitutes (different product, same need)
- Indirect substitutes (different approach entirely)
- "Do nothing" as a substitute (how painful is the status quo?)
- Price-performance of substitutes vs. your approach
- *The key question:* Could the customer solve this problem a completely different way?

**5. Competitive Rivalry** (How intense is current competition?)
- Number and size of competitors
- Growth rate of the market (growing markets reduce rivalry)
- Differentiation between competitors (commodity vs. differentiated)
- Exit barriers (do losers stay in the market anyway?)
- Competitive behavior patterns (price wars? feature wars? marketing wars?)
- *The key question:* Is this a fight over market share, or is the pie growing fast enough for everyone?

### Common mistakes
- Treating it as a checklist instead of analyzing the INTERACTIONS between forces
- Ignoring the most important insight: which 1-2 forces dominate and what that implies
- Not connecting the analysis to a "so what" for the specific decision at hand

### Output format
Analyze all 5 forces, then synthesize: "The dominant forces in this space are [X] and [Y], which means [implication]. This matters for the research question because [connection]."

## Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) {#jobs-to-be-done}

### What it is
Analyzes what "job" the customer is hiring a product/service to do. Shifts focus from product features to customer outcomes.

### When to use
- Evaluating product-market fit
- Understanding why customers choose competitors
- Identifying underserved needs
- Feature prioritization research
- Understanding the real reason customers buy (vs. the stated reason)

### How to apply it properly

**Step 1: Identify the functional job**
What task is the customer trying to accomplish? Be specific and outcome-oriented.
- Bad: "The customer wants a disability parking placard"
- Good: "The customer wants to park close to building entrances because walking long distances causes pain that limits their daily activities"

**Step 2: Identify the emotional job**
How does the customer want to FEEL during and after?
- Validated (not judged for needing help)
- In control (not lost in bureaucracy)
- Relieved (the problem is handled)
- Confident (it's done correctly and won't be challenged)

**Step 3: Identify the social job**
How does the customer want to be PERCEIVED?
- Not seen as gaming the system
- Seen as responsible and proactive about their health
- Not pitied, but accommodated

**Step 4: Map the consumption chain**
What happens before, during, and after the "job"?
1. How does the customer first realize they need this?
2. How do they search for solutions?
3. How do they evaluate options?
4. How do they purchase/start?
5. How do they use it?
6. How do they maintain/renew?
7. How do they stop using it or switch?

At each stage, identify: friction points, anxiety points, and moments of delight.

**Step 5: Identify competing solutions**
What else do customers "hire" for the same job? Include non-obvious competitors:
- Direct competitors (other companies offering the same thing)
- DIY solutions (doing it themselves, even if harder)
- Workarounds (imperfect substitutes)
- Non-consumption (just living with the problem)

### Common mistakes
- Defining the job too narrowly (feature-level) or too broadly (life-level)
- Ignoring emotional and social jobs (these often drive decisions more than functional jobs)
- Not mapping the full consumption chain (the job doesn't start at purchase)

### Output format
Present as a Job Statement: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]." Then map the consumption chain with friction/anxiety/delight at each stage.

## Competitive Positioning Map {#competitive-positioning-map}

### What it is
A 2D visual framework that plots competitors along two dimensions that matter most to customers, revealing gaps and opportunities.

### When to use
- Competitive analysis
- Finding whitespace in a market
- Understanding positioning options
- Evaluating where to compete

### How to apply it properly

**Step 1: Identify the 2 dimensions that matter most**
These should be the primary axes of customer decision-making. Common pairs:
- Price vs. Quality/Comprehensiveness
- Speed vs. Thoroughness
- Specialization vs. Breadth
- Self-service vs. Full-service
- Online vs. In-person

The RIGHT dimensions are the ones where competitors actually differ AND customers actually care. If everyone's in the same quadrant, pick different dimensions.

**Step 2: Plot every competitor**
Place each competitor on the map with a brief note on why they're positioned there. Use evidence (pricing data, feature sets, customer reviews) not assumptions.

**Step 3: Identify clusters and gaps**
- Where are competitors clustered? (Red ocean — intense competition)
- Where are the empty spaces? (Potential whitespace)
- For empty spaces, ask: Is this empty because it's unattractive, or because nobody's tried?

**Step 4: Plot the user's current/intended position**
Where does the user's product/service sit? Where SHOULD it sit?

### Common mistakes
- Choosing dimensions that don't actually differentiate competitors
- Not validating that customers care about the chosen dimensions
- Assuming empty whitespace is always an opportunity (sometimes it's empty for good reason)

## SWOT (Done Properly) {#swot-done-properly}

### What it is
Analyzes Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. One of the most overused and underperformed frameworks — powerful when done right, useless when done generically.

### When to use
- Evaluating a specific opportunity or decision
- Assessing competitive position
- Strategic planning

### How to apply it properly (the non-lazy version)

The secret to a useful SWOT: everything must be SPECIFIC and COMPARATIVE.

**Strengths** — not "good team" but "3 physicians licensed in all 50 states, which competitors X and Y don't have." Every strength should answer: "Stronger than WHO, and by HOW MUCH?"

**Weaknesses** — not "limited marketing budget" but "spending $X/month on Google Ads vs. competitor Y's estimated $Z/month, resulting in 40% less search visibility for key terms." Every weakness should answer: "Weaker than WHO, and what's the IMPACT?"

**Opportunities** — not "growing market" but "the disability parking placard market has X applications per year growing at Y%, and Z% are currently processed online, leaving an addressable gap of [number]." Every opportunity should answer: "How BIG is this, and what would it take to CAPTURE it?"

**Threats** — not "regulatory risk" but "SB-1234 in California would require in-person examinations for parking placards, which would eliminate our telehealth model in a state representing X% of revenue." Every threat should answer: "How LIKELY is this, how SEVERE would it be, and what's the MITIGATION?"

**After completing the 4 quadrants, build the Strategy Matrix:**
- SO strategies: Use strengths to capture opportunities
- WO strategies: Fix weaknesses to unlock opportunities
- ST strategies: Use strengths to defend against threats
- WT strategies: Mitigate weaknesses that make threats more dangerous

### Common mistakes
- Being vague and generic (the #1 failure mode)
- Not making it comparative (strengths/weaknesses are relative, not absolute)
- Listing without prioritizing (rank items by impact)
- Skipping the strategy matrix (the whole point is the SO/WO/ST/WT intersection)

## Value Proposition Canvas {#value-proposition-canvas}

### What it is
Maps the fit between what you offer (value map) and what the customer needs (customer profile). Useful for evaluating product-market fit.

### When to use
- Evaluating new product or service ideas
- Understanding why a product is or isn't resonating
- Comparing value propositions of competitors
- Feature prioritization

### How to apply it properly

**Customer Profile (Right Side):**
1. **Customer Jobs:** What tasks are they trying to accomplish? (Use JTBD framework)
2. **Pains:** What frustrations, obstacles, and risks do they experience?
3. **Gains:** What outcomes and benefits do they desire?

**Value Map (Left Side):**
1. **Products/Services:** What do you offer?
2. **Pain Relievers:** How do your offerings alleviate specific pains?
3. **Gain Creators:** How do your offerings create specific gains?

**The Fit Analysis:**
Draw explicit lines between pains → pain relievers and gains → gain creators. Where there's a strong match, you have fit. Where there are unaddressed pains or uncreated gains, you have opportunity or risk.

## Technology Adoption Lifecycle {#technology-adoption-lifecycle}

### What it is
Maps where a technology or product category sits on the adoption curve: Innovators → Early Adopters → Early Majority → Late Majority → Laggards. Includes the critical "chasm" between early adopters and early majority.

### When to use
- Evaluating market timing
- Understanding why adoption is stalling
- Deciding marketing and sales approach
- Technology or product category research

### How to apply it properly

**Determine current position on the curve by analyzing:**
- What % of the addressable market is currently using this? (<5% = innovators/early adopters, 5-25% = crossing the chasm, 25-50% = early majority, >50% = late majority)
- What type of customer is buying? (Visionaries and tech enthusiasts vs. pragmatists vs. conservatives)
- What are the buying objections? (Early: "does it work?" Middle: "who else uses it?" Late: "is it required?")
- Is there a "whole product" available? (Does the customer need to assemble pieces, or is it turnkey?)

**The Chasm Analysis:**
If the product/market is between early adopters and early majority, focus on:
- What's the "whole product" gap? (What else does the customer need besides the core product?)
- Is there a beachhead segment? (A specific niche where the whole product CAN be delivered?)
- What references exist? (Early majority wants proof from people like them, not from visionaries)

## Regulatory Landscape Mapping {#regulatory-landscape-mapping}

### What it is
A systematic approach to understanding the regulatory environment for a product, service, or market.

### When to use
- Any healthcare or telehealth research
- Entering regulated industries
- State-by-state compliance analysis
- Evaluating regulatory risk

### How to apply it properly

**Layer 1: Federal Framework**
- Which federal agencies have jurisdiction? (FDA, FTC, CMS, DEA, etc.)
- What federal laws apply? (Specific statutes, not vague "federal regulation")
- Current enforcement posture (aggressive, passive, evolving?)
- Recent enforcement actions in this space

**Layer 2: State-by-State Analysis**
For each relevant state:
- Licensing requirements
- Telehealth-specific rules (if applicable)
- Scope of practice limitations
- Recent legislative activity
- Enforcement history

**Layer 3: Enforcement Reality**
The written law and actual enforcement often differ. Research:
- What do enforcement actions actually target? (Read FTC complaints, state AG actions)
- What's the complaint-to-action ratio? (How likely is enforcement?)
- Are there safe harbors or established practices that aren't technically codified?
- What's the trend? (Increasing scrutiny? New enforcement priorities?)

**Layer 4: Regulatory Trajectory**
- Pending legislation or rulemaking
- Industry comment letters (reveal what the industry is worried about)
- Analogous regulatory actions in adjacent spaces
- Political signals (new administration priorities, committee hearings)

### Output format
Present as a regulatory risk matrix: [Regulation] × [Likelihood of Enforcement] × [Severity if Enforced] × [Mitigation Available]

## Market Segmentation Framework {#market-segmentation-framework}

### What it is
Divides a market into distinct groups based on shared characteristics, needs, or behaviors.

### When to use
- Market entry research
- Identifying target segments
- Understanding different customer groups
- Sizing addressable market

### How to apply it properly

**Step 1: Identify segmentation variables**
Choose variables that actually predict behavior (not just demographics):
- **Needs-based:** What problem are they solving? How urgent is it?
- **Behavioral:** How do they currently solve the problem? What's their buying process?
- **Value-based:** How much is the solution worth to them? What's their willingness to pay?
- **Demographic/firmographic:** Age, location, company size (only useful when correlated with needs)

**Step 2: Size each segment**
For each segment, estimate:
- Total addressable market (TAM): Everyone who COULD buy
- Serviceable addressable market (SAM): Everyone you COULD reach
- Serviceable obtainable market (SOM): What you could realistically capture in 1-2 years

**Step 3: Evaluate attractiveness**
Score each segment on: size, growth rate, profitability, competition intensity, accessibility, fit with capabilities.

## Root Cause Analysis {#root-cause-analysis}

### What it is
Identifies the fundamental cause of a problem by systematically drilling past symptoms to reach root causes.

### When to use
- Problem-solving research
- Understanding why something isn't working
- Diagnosing operational issues
- Analyzing failures or underperformance

### How to apply it properly

**5 Whys Technique:**
Start with the problem statement. Ask "why?" 5 times, each time digging one level deeper.

Example:
1. Why are conversion rates low? → Cart abandonment is high
2. Why is cart abandonment high? → Users drop off at the payment step
3. Why do users drop off at payment? → The payment form requires information they don't have ready
4. Why don't they have the information ready? → They didn't know the cost until the final step
5. Why don't they know the cost? → Pricing isn't shown until after the consultation is scheduled

Root cause: Price transparency issue, not a "conversion rate" issue.

**Ishikawa (Fishbone) for complex problems:**
When there are multiple potential causes, organize them by category:
- People (skills, training, staffing)
- Process (workflow, procedures, handoffs)
- Technology (tools, systems, integrations)
- Policy (rules, requirements, constraints)
- External (market, regulatory, competitive)

### Common mistakes
- Stopping at the first "why" (symptoms, not causes)
- Assuming a single root cause when there are multiple contributing factors
- Not validating the root cause with evidence

## Analogous Industry Scan {#analogous-industry-scan}

### What it is
Looks at how OTHER industries solved similar problems, revealing solutions that haven't been tried in the target industry.

### When to use
- Problem-solving research (especially when the industry seems "stuck")
- Innovation research
- Feature/product ideation
- When conventional approaches aren't working

### How to apply it properly

**Step 1: Abstract the problem**
Remove industry-specific details to find the underlying challenge.
- Industry-specific: "How do we get more disability parking placard applications?"
- Abstracted: "How do you increase applications for government-validated credentials that require medical documentation?"

**Step 2: Identify analogous industries**
What other industries face the same abstracted problem?
- Medical marijuana cards (same structure: medical documentation → government credential)
- ESA letters (similar: clinical evaluation → documentation → access to benefit)
- Insurance pre-authorizations (medical documentation → approval → access to service)
- Professional licensing (documentation → credential → access to practice)

**Step 3: Research how those industries solved it**
What worked? What failed? What's transferable? What's specific to their context?

**Step 4: Translate back**
Apply the insights, adjusting for the specific constraints of the target industry.

### Common mistakes
- Choosing analogies that are too similar (same industry, different product) or too different (no structural overlap)
- Not adjusting for context when translating solutions back
- Cherry-picking only successful analogies (failures teach more)
