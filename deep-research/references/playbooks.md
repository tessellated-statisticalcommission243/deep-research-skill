# Research Playbooks

Step-by-step research playbooks for common research categories. Each playbook provides a specific execution sequence, framework selection, source priorities, and output emphasis.

Identify which playbook(s) match the current research topic. If a topic spans multiple categories, combine the relevant playbooks — use the primary playbook as the backbone and pull specific techniques from secondary playbooks.

## Market Entry / New Vertical Research {#market-entry}

**Use when:** Evaluating whether to enter a new market, launch a new brand, or expand into a new vertical.

**Examples:** "Should we launch a NAD+ therapy service?" "Is there a market for telehealth-based FMLA certifications?" "What does the GLP-1 weight loss market look like?"

### Execution Sequence

**Phase 1: Market Sizing & Validation (Layer 1 + Layer 2)**

Step 1 — Size the market using three independent approaches:
- **Top-down:** Start with total addressable market data from industry reports. Note the methodology and what's included/excluded.
- **Bottom-up:** Estimate from unit economics. How many potential customers × average transaction value × purchase frequency.
- **Analogous:** Find a similar market that's more mature. What's the penetration rate there? Apply a similar rate to this market.
- If the three approaches diverge significantly, investigate why. The discrepancy itself is informative.

Step 2 — Map the competitive landscape:
- Identify all players (direct and indirect competitors, substitutes, DIY alternatives)
- Apply the Competitive Positioning Map framework
- Note market concentration (is it fragmented or dominated by a few players?)

Step 3 — Understand the customer:
- Apply JTBD framework to understand what the customer is actually trying to accomplish
- Map the current customer journey (how do they solve this problem today?)
- Identify the biggest friction points in the current journey

**Phase 2: Structural Analysis (Layer 2 + Layer 3)**

Step 4 — Apply PESTEL to understand macro forces:
- Focus on the 2-3 dimensions most relevant to this market
- Pay special attention to regulatory trajectory (tightening or loosening?)

Step 5 — Apply Porter's Five Forces:
- Assess competitive intensity and profitability dynamics
- Identify the dominant force(s) — this shapes the entry strategy

Step 6 — Analyze business models in the space:
- How do existing players make money? (Direct sales, subscription, insurance, B2B2C?)
- What are typical margins?
- What are the unit economics of the most successful players?

**Phase 3: Depth & Validation (Layer 3)**

Step 7 — Research implementation reality:
- What does it actually take to launch in this market? (Licensing, technology, partnerships, marketing)
- What's the typical time-to-revenue?
- What are the hidden costs that aren't obvious from the outside?

Step 8 — Find failure cases:
- Who tried to enter this market and failed? Why?
- What are the most common reasons for failure?
- Are there structural barriers that aren't visible from the outside?

Step 9 — Talk to the edges:
- What do power users/early adopters say about the space?
- What do skeptics and critics say?
- What does the regulator say vs. what do practitioners do?

**Phase 4: Opportunity Assessment (Layer 4 + Layer 5)**

Step 10 — Identify adjacent opportunities
Step 11 — Assess timing (Technology Adoption Lifecycle position)
Step 12 — Build the decision framework

### Frameworks to use
Primary: PESTEL + Porter's Five Forces + Market Segmentation
Secondary: JTBD + Competitive Positioning Map + Technology Adoption Lifecycle

### Source priority
Industry reports > Financial data > Competitor intelligence > Practitioner forums > Government sources > News

### Output emphasis
Heavy on Layer 2 (landscape) and Layer 3 (implementation reality). Decision Points section is critical — frame as go/no-go with specific conditions.

## Product & Feature Research {#product-feature}

**Use when:** Evaluating new features, products, or tools to build or buy. Understanding how a specific product category works in practice.

**Examples:** "How should we build an internal referral feature?" "What loyalty/referral systems exist?" "How do medical records features work in patient portals?"

### Execution Sequence

**Phase 1: Problem Definition (Layer 1)**

Step 1 — Define the problem precisely:
- Apply Root Cause Analysis (5 Whys) to get past the surface request
- The user might say "we need a referral feature" but the real problem might be "customer acquisition cost is too high" or "organic growth has plateaued"
- Get to the root problem before researching solutions

Step 2 — Apply JTBD:
- What job is the user (or their customer) trying to accomplish?
- Map the full consumption chain
- Identify where current solutions fail

**Phase 2: Solution Landscape (Layer 2)**

Step 3 — Map the solution categories:
- Build vs. Buy vs. Integrate decision framework
- For Buy: What vendors exist? Apply Competitive Positioning Map.
- For Build: What are the common architectural approaches?
- For Integrate: What APIs, plugins, or platforms offer this?

Step 4 — Evaluate each approach:
For each option, document:
- Core capabilities
- Pricing model and total cost of ownership (including implementation, maintenance, opportunity cost)
- Integration requirements
- Limitations and what it DOESN'T do
- Customer reviews and real-world feedback (especially negative reviews)

**Phase 3: Implementation Reality (Layer 3)**

Step 5 — Research implementations:
- How have similar companies implemented this?
- What were the gotchas?
- What took longer/cost more than expected?
- What did they wish they'd known before starting?

Step 6 — Technical deep dive:
- What's the technical complexity?
- What dependencies does it create?
- How does it interact with existing systems?
- What's the maintenance burden?

Step 7 — User experience research:
- How do end users actually use this feature in competing products?
- What do they love? What do they hate?
- What's the adoption curve? (Do users actually use it after the first week?)

**Phase 4: Recommendation Framework (Layer 4)**

Step 8 — Build the decision matrix:
- Score options across dimensions that matter: cost, time to implement, quality, flexibility, risk
- Weight dimensions by importance for this specific context
- Present as a clear comparison table

Step 9 — Identify adjacencies:
- What other features or improvements become possible once this is built?
- What second-order effects should be anticipated?

### Frameworks to use
Primary: JTBD + Root Cause Analysis + Value Proposition Canvas
Secondary: Competitive Positioning Map + Analogous Industry Scan

### Source priority
Competitor products > Practitioner forums > User reviews > Technical documentation > Industry analysis

### Output emphasis
Heavy on Layer 3 (implementation reality). The decision matrix is the centerpiece. The reader should be able to pick an approach based on the output.

## Competitive Analysis {#competitive-analysis}

**Use when:** Understanding competitive dynamics, evaluating a competitor's strategy, or finding competitive advantages.

**Examples:** "Who are our main competitors and how are they positioned?" "What is Company X doing differently?" "Where are the competitive gaps we can exploit?"

### Execution Sequence

**Phase 1: Competitor Identification (Layer 1)**

Step 1 — Build the competitive set:
- Direct competitors (same product/service, same customer)
- Indirect competitors (different product, same customer need)
- Substitute competitors (completely different approach to same problem)
- Future competitors (companies adjacent to the space that could enter)

Step 2 — Basic intelligence on each:
- Company size, funding, team size (LinkedIn, Crunchbase, job postings)
- Product/service offering
- Pricing model and price points
- Target customer segment

**Phase 2: Strategic Analysis (Layer 2)**

Step 3 — Apply Competitive Positioning Map:
- Choose 2 dimensions that matter most to customers
- Plot all competitors
- Identify clusters and whitespace

Step 4 — Analyze each significant competitor:
- Value proposition (what they promise)
- Business model (how they make money)
- Go-to-market strategy (how they acquire customers)
- Technology approach (what their stack looks like — check BuiltWith, job postings)
- Strengths and vulnerabilities

Step 5 — Apply Porter's Five Forces:
- How are competitive dynamics likely to evolve?
- What would change the competitive landscape?

**Phase 3: Deep Intelligence (Layer 3)**

Step 6 — Customer intelligence:
- Read every 1-star, 2-star, and 3-star review for major competitors (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, BBB, App Store)
- Categorize complaints: product issues, service issues, pricing issues, trust issues
- Identify patterns (what are competitors consistently failing at?)

Step 7 — Operational intelligence:
- Job postings (what are they hiring for? This reveals strategic priorities)
- Press releases and blog posts (what are they announcing? What are they NOT announcing?)
- Social media engagement (how do their customers talk about them?)
- Partnership and integration announcements

Step 8 — Marketing and SEO intelligence:
- What keywords do they rank for? (SimilarWeb, SEMrush signals from search results)
- What's their content strategy?
- Where do they advertise? What's their messaging?
- What's their conversion funnel like? (Sign up and walk through it if possible)

Step 9 — Financial intelligence (where available):
- Revenue estimates (for public companies: filings; for private: triangulate from team size, pricing, review volume)
- Growth rate signals
- Funding history and investor expectations

**Phase 4: Strategic Implications (Layer 4)**

Step 10 — Identify competitive advantages:
- What can we do that they can't? (or vice versa)
- What would be hard for them to copy?
- Where are they overserving (and therefore overcharging)?
- Where are they underserving (and therefore leaving opportunity)?

Step 11 — Predict competitive moves:
- Based on their hiring, funding, and product trajectory, what are they likely to do next?
- What would a rational competitor do given their position?

### Frameworks to use
Primary: Competitive Positioning Map + Porter's Five Forces + SWOT (done properly)
Secondary: Value Proposition Canvas + JTBD

### Source priority
Competitor intelligence > User reviews > Financial data > Job postings > Industry reports > News

### Output emphasis
Heavy on all layers. The Competitive Positioning Map is the visual centerpiece. Customer complaint patterns are often the most valuable finding.

## Regulatory & Compliance Research {#regulatory}

**Use when:** Understanding regulatory requirements, assessing compliance risk, or researching state-by-state variations.

**Examples:** "What are the telehealth requirements for disability parking permits across states?" "What are the regulations for GLP-1 prescriptions via telehealth?" "What compliance requirements apply to FMLA certifications?"

### Execution Sequence

**Phase 1: Federal Framework (Layer 1 + Layer 2)**

Step 1 — Identify governing federal agencies and laws
Step 2 — Map current federal regulations (specific statutes and rules, not vague references)
Step 3 — Assess federal enforcement posture (recent actions, guidance documents, enforcement priorities)

**Phase 2: State-Level Analysis (Layer 2 + Layer 3)**

Step 4 — Identify which states are relevant
Step 5 — For each relevant state, research:
- Specific regulatory requirements (cite the actual statute/rule)
- Licensing requirements for practitioners
- Telehealth-specific provisions or limitations
- Recent legislative activity (pending bills, recently passed laws)
- Enforcement history (has anyone been sanctioned for non-compliance?)

Step 6 — Build the state comparison matrix:
Organize findings into a state × requirement matrix that allows quick comparison

**Phase 3: Enforcement Reality (Layer 3)**

Step 7 — Research enforcement patterns:
- What do regulators actually enforce vs. what's technically on the books?
- What triggers investigations? (Complaints? Audits? Random? Political?)
- What are the penalties in practice? (Fines? License revocation? Criminal?)
- Are there precedent cases that establish boundaries?

Step 8 — Research practitioner behavior:
- How do current practitioners interpret ambiguous regulations?
- What informal "safe harbors" exist?
- Where is the line between common practice and potential violation?

Step 9 — Research industry compliance approaches:
- How do competitors handle compliance?
- What compliance infrastructure is standard? (Legal opinions, compliance officers, audit procedures)

**Phase 4: Trajectory & Risk Assessment (Layer 4 + Layer 5)**

Step 10 — Assess regulatory trajectory:
- Apply PESTEL (Political + Legal dimensions)
- What pending legislation could change the landscape?
- What regulatory trends from adjacent industries might spill over?

Step 11 — Build the risk matrix:
For each regulation: [Likelihood of enforcement] × [Severity if enforced] × [Mitigation available] × [Cost of compliance]

Step 12 — Identify regulatory arbitrage opportunities:
- Where is regulation favorable?
- Where are regulations creating barriers that benefit compliant players?

### Frameworks to use
Primary: Regulatory Landscape Mapping + PESTEL
Secondary: Analogous Industry Scan (how do similar industries handle similar regulations?)

### Source priority
Government sources (.gov) > Court filings > Enforcement actions > Industry guidance > Legal commentary > Practitioner forums > News

### Output emphasis
The state comparison matrix and risk matrix are the centerpieces. Enforcement reality (what actually happens vs. what's written) is the most valuable Layer 3 content.

## Problem-Solving Research {#problem-solving}

**Use when:** The user has a specific problem and wants to understand how to solve it, or wants to understand how others have solved it.

**Examples:** "How do we improve conversion rates?" "How do we reduce customer acquisition cost?" "How should we approach customer retention?"

### Execution Sequence

**Phase 1: Problem Definition (Layer 1)**

Step 1 — Apply Root Cause Analysis:
- Use 5 Whys to get past the surface problem
- Use Ishikawa to map all contributing factors
- Validate: Is this the right problem to solve, or is there a bigger problem upstream?

Step 2 — Quantify the problem:
- How big is the problem? (Revenue impact, time impact, customer impact)
- How long has it existed?
- What's been tried before? What worked? What didn't?

**Phase 2: Solution Landscape (Layer 2)**

Step 3 — Map all known solutions:
- What do experts recommend?
- What have similar companies done?
- What are the conventional approaches?

Step 4 — Apply Analogous Industry Scan:
- Abstract the problem to its core structure
- Find analogous industries that solved a similar problem
- Research their solutions and results
- Translate insights back to the specific context

**Phase 3: Case Studies & Evidence (Layer 3)**

Step 5 — Find 3-5 relevant case studies:
- Companies of similar size/stage that faced the same problem
- Prefer specific, detailed case studies over generic "best practices"
- Include at least 1 failure case (what DIDN'T work)

Step 6 — Extract lessons from each case:
- What was the specific approach?
- What were the results (with numbers)?
- What were the preconditions for success?
- What went wrong (or what was harder than expected)?
- Is this transferable to the user's context?

Step 7 — Research implementation details:
- What tools/technology did they use?
- What was the timeline?
- What did it cost (money, time, opportunity cost)?
- What skills/people were required?

**Phase 4: Solution Framework (Layer 4)**

Step 8 — Build the solution comparison:
- Score each approach on: effectiveness (evidence), cost, time to implement, risk, fit with current operations
- Identify quick wins vs. strategic bets
- Note dependencies and prerequisites

Step 9 — Identify adjacent improvements:
- What other problems would get easier if this problem is solved?
- What problems should be solved FIRST because they're prerequisites?
- What "free" improvements come along with solving this problem?

### Frameworks to use
Primary: Root Cause Analysis + Analogous Industry Scan
Secondary: JTBD (to understand the problem from the customer's perspective) + Value Proposition Canvas

### Source priority
Practitioner forums > Case studies > Industry reports > Academic research > Competitor intelligence > News

### Output emphasis
Heavy on Layer 3 (real case studies with specifics). The solution comparison matrix is the centerpiece. Quick wins vs. strategic bets distinction is critical.

## Service Expansion Research {#service-expansion}

**Use when:** Evaluating new services or offerings to add to an existing brand or platform. Differs from Market Entry in that there's already an established customer base and brand.

**Examples:** "Should ParkingMD offer utility discounts or tax exemptions?" "Should we add NAD+ therapy to the Minimal brand?" "What services could we add to the patient portal?"

### Execution Sequence

**Phase 1: Opportunity Identification (Layer 1 + Layer 2)**

Step 1 — Map the full universe of possible services:
- What does the existing customer base need beyond what we already offer?
- What services share the same regulatory framework?
- What services share the same customer acquisition channel?
- What are competitors offering that we're not?

Step 2 — First-pass screening:
For each potential service, quick-score on:
- Customer demand signal (is there evidence people want this?)
- Regulatory feasibility (can we legally offer it?)
- Operational fit (can we deliver it with existing infrastructure?)
- Revenue potential (is it big enough to matter?)
- Competitive landscape (how crowded is it?)

Eliminate services that fail on any critical dimension. Deep-dive the survivors.

**Phase 2: Deep Dive on Survivors (Layer 2 + Layer 3)**

Step 3 — For each surviving service:
- Size the opportunity (TAM, SAM, SOM)
- Map the competitive landscape
- Understand the delivery requirements
- Research regulatory requirements
- Identify the customer journey and how it connects to existing services

Step 4 — Cross-sell analysis:
- What % of existing customers would be interested?
- How does this service connect to the existing customer journey?
- Does offering this service increase retention or lifetime value?
- Does it create a referral loop?

Step 5 — Operational analysis:
- What new capabilities are needed? (Physicians, technology, licenses, partnerships)
- Can existing infrastructure support this?
- What's the marginal cost of adding this vs. the incremental revenue?

**Phase 3: Risk Assessment (Layer 3)**

Step 6 — Research what can go wrong:
- Regulatory risk specific to this service
- Brand risk (does this dilute or strengthen the brand?)
- Operational risk (does this distract from core services?)
- Customer experience risk (does this make the product confusing?)

Step 7 — Find precedents:
- Who else expanded into this service? How did it go?
- Are there failure cases to learn from?

**Phase 4: Prioritization (Layer 4)**

Step 8 — Build the expansion priority matrix:
Score each service on: revenue potential, strategic fit, operational complexity, time to launch, risk
Plot on a 2×2: [Revenue Potential] × [Ease of Implementation]

Step 9 — Sequence the expansion:
- What should be launched first, second, third?
- Are there dependencies between services?
- What's the learning plan? (Which services test assumptions needed for later services?)

### Frameworks to use
Primary: Value Proposition Canvas + SWOT + Market Segmentation
Secondary: JTBD + Competitive Positioning Map + Business Model Canvas

### Source priority
Competitor intelligence > Government sources > User demand signals > Industry reports > Practitioner forums

### Output emphasis
The expansion priority matrix is the centerpiece. Cross-sell analysis and operational fit assessment are the most valuable Layer 3 content. The sequencing recommendation ties it together.
