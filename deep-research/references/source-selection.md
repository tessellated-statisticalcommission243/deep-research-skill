# Source Selection Guide

Sources are not equal. Their weight depends on the topic being researched. This guide maps topic categories to source priorities.

---

## How to Use This Guide

1. Identify which category (or categories) the research topic falls into
2. Use the priority sources for that category as primary research targets
3. Use secondary sources to fill gaps and cross-validate
4. Check for red flags in all sources

---

## Source Categories

### Government & Regulatory Sources
**High-weight for:** Regulatory research, compliance questions, public benefit programs, licensing, tax topics, disability services, healthcare policy
**Includes:** Federal/state .gov sites, regulatory agency publications, legislative databases, court opinions, enforcement action records, Federal Register, CMS/HHS guidance
**Strength:** Authoritative, primary source, legally binding
**Weakness:** Often outdated, hard to navigate, may not reflect actual enforcement
**Signal boost:** Cross-reference with enforcement actions and practitioner experiences to understand rules as applied vs. as written

### Industry & Trade Sources
**High-weight for:** Market entry, industry trends, competitive landscape, B2B product evaluation
**Includes:** Industry associations, trade publications, market research firms (IBISWorld, Statista, Grand View Research), conference proceedings, trade journals
**Strength:** Industry-specific context, trend data, benchmarks
**Weakness:** Often paywalled, may have industry bias, market sizing methodologies vary
**Signal boost:** Always check methodology notes in market sizing. Two reports with different numbers reveal more than one report alone.

### Practitioner & Community Sources
**High-weight for:** Implementation research, product evaluation, "how does this actually work" questions
**Includes:** Reddit (specific subreddits), Hacker News, Stack Overflow, niche forums, Facebook groups, Discord communities, ProductHunt discussions
**Strength:** Real-world experience, unfiltered pain points, failure stories
**Weakness:** Anecdotal, may be outdated, selection bias
**Signal boost:** Look for patterns across multiple accounts. Weight detailed, specific experiences over vague opinions. Check dates.

### Competitor & Product Intelligence
**High-weight for:** Competitive analysis, feature research, pricing, positioning
**Includes:** Competitor websites, G2/Capterra/TrustPilot reviews, App Store reviews, competitor blogs, job postings, press releases, LinkedIn, Crunchbase, PitchBook
**Strength:** Direct signal on competitor behavior and customer sentiment
**Weakness:** Marketing ≠ reality, reviews can be gamed
**Signal boost:** Job postings reveal strategy. Negative reviews reveal pain points. "Contact us" pricing signals enterprise focus. Changelogs reveal velocity.

### Academic & Research Sources
**High-weight for:** Healthcare research, evidence-based claims, regulatory impact, technology fundamentals
**Includes:** PubMed, Google Scholar, university research, NIH/CDC publications, peer-reviewed journals, systematic reviews
**Strength:** Rigorous methodology, peer-reviewed
**Weakness:** Slow to publish, may be too theoretical
**Signal boost:** Systematic reviews > individual studies. Check citation counts, recency, and the "limitations" section.

### News & Media Sources
**High-weight for:** Recent events, funding news, regulatory changes, public perception
**Includes:** Major outlets, TechCrunch, healthcare trade news (Fierce Healthcare, STAT News), local news
**Strength:** Timely, contextual
**Weakness:** Surface-level, may sensationalize
**Signal boost:** Use for recency, verify against primary sources. Local news is underrated for state-specific stories.

### Financial & Business Data
**High-weight for:** Market sizing, company evaluation, business model analysis
**Includes:** SEC filings, annual reports, investor presentations, Crunchbase, PitchBook, SimilarWeb, BuiltWith
**Strength:** Hard numbers, verified for public companies
**Weakness:** Limited for private companies, lagging
**Signal boost:** Investor presentations are more honest than marketing. 10-K "Risk Factors" are goldmines of real concerns.

### User & Customer Sources
**High-weight for:** Product research, feature validation, pain point identification
**Includes:** App Store reviews, G2/Capterra, BBB complaints, social media, customer forums
**Strength:** Authentic user voice, reveals real problems
**Weakness:** Selection bias, may be outdated
**Signal boost:** 1-star and 2-star reviews contain more signal than 5-star. Look for recurring themes across time periods.

## Cross-Category Research Matrix

| Research Type | Primary Sources | Secondary Sources | Low-Value Sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| New market entry | Industry + Financial + Competitor | Practitioner + News | Academic (usually) |
| Regulatory compliance | Government + Academic | News + Practitioner | Competitor |
| Feature/product research | Competitor + User + Practitioner | Industry + News | Government (usually) |
| Problem solving | Practitioner + Competitor + Academic | Industry + News | Financial (usually) |
| Competitive analysis | Competitor + User + Financial | News + Industry | Academic (usually) |
| Healthcare/telehealth | Government + Academic + Industry | Practitioner + News | General user reviews |
| Service expansion | Competitor + Government + User | Industry + Practitioner | Academic (usually) |

## Source Red Flags

Deprioritize or flag with low confidence:
- Content farms and SEO-optimized listicles (high volume, low signal)
- Undated content with no author attribution
- Sources with obvious commercial bias toward a specific product
- Market research from unknown firms with suspiciously precise numbers
- Social media posts from accounts that only promote one product
- "Expert" roundups that are link-building exercises
- Any source that makes claims without citing its own sources

---

## When Sources Conflict

1. State the disagreement explicitly in the output
2. Weight by source type (primary > secondary > tertiary)
3. Weight by recency (newer usually wins, unless the older source is more rigorous)
4. Weight by specificity (exact topic > related topic)
5. Weight by incentive alignment (who benefits from each claim being true?)
6. Present both sides and flag the conflict for the reader
